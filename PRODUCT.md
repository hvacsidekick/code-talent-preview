# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Code Talent's site speaks to two audiences at once and must serve both without diluting either:

- **Hiring side** — founders, CTOs, hiring leaders, and startup operators at Colorado and national technology companies, from seed-stage teams making their first engineering hire to pre-IPO companies scaling a growth org. They arrive skeptical of recruiters, short on time, and looking for proof that this partner actually understands technical roles and the local market.
- **Talent side** — senior technical candidates (engineering, data, product, design, infrastructure, leadership) weighing a career move and wanting to be handled with context and discretion, not blasted with volume.
- **Community side** — Colorado tech community organizers and meetup hosts who know Code Talent as a long-standing supporter of the local scene and a place to host events.

Context of use: mostly desktop during a workday research/vetting session, with a meaningful mobile minority. Visitors are comparing Code Talent against other agencies and against hiring in-house, so credibility and specificity win over polish-for-polish's-sake.

## Product Purpose

Position Code Talent as the Denver-rooted, community-powered recruiting partner for standout technical teams and standout candidates. The site exists to convert skeptical hiring leaders and high-caliber candidates into conversations, and to make Code Talent's decade of Colorado community investment legible as a genuine competitive moat rather than a footnote. Success looks like: a hiring leader books an intro, a strong candidate joins the talent network, and a community organizer reaches out to host a meetup — each leaving convinced this is a firm that is unusually plugged in, human, and exacting.

## Brand Personality

Elite, connected, human, exacting, high-trust, locally rooted. The voice is confident and specific — it names real companies, real communities, and real process instead of hiding behind adjectives. It is warm without being casual, and precise without being cold. Three words: **plugged-in, exacting, human.** The `code.` wordmark — lowercase, monospace, with a trailing period — is the anchor of the identity: technical, deliberate, quietly self-assured.

## Anti-references

Explicitly avoid looking like any of these:

- Generic SaaS landing pages (gradient-mesh hero, feature-triplet cards, "trusted by" logo wall as the only proof).
- Recruiter and staffing-agency stock-photo templates (handshakes, headset call-center imagery, skyline clip art).
- Bland identical card grids — same-sized icon-heading-text cards repeated down the page.
- Fake or unverifiable metrics ("10,000+ placements", invented satisfaction percentages).
- Overdone "AI editorial" layouts — display-serif-italic headlines, drop caps, ruled three-column broadsheet grids applied to a brief that isn't a magazine.
- Decorative motion with no purpose — looping attention-seekers, bounce/elastic easing, fade-in-on-scroll applied uniformly to every section.

## Design Principles

1. **Community is the moat — show it, don't claim it.** The meetups/community section is a primary proof asset, not a footnote: lead with a live agenda, name every supported group, and make a decade of local investment visible and verifiable.
2. **Specific beats superlative.** Real company names, real role categories, real process steps, and confirmed facts (since 2013, Denver Startup Week tracks, 21 supported groups) carry the credibility. Never invent a number.
3. **Two journeys, one firm.** Hiring leaders and candidates get distinct, legible paths without fracturing the brand or duplicating the whole site.
4. **Editorial rhythm over template repetition.** Vary section structure, spacing, and emphasis so the page reads as authored, not assembled from one card component.
5. **Motion serves orientation and trust.** Every animation earns its place through orientation, state feedback, focus, or a single premium first-load moment — and every one is reduced-motion-safe.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Responsive and readable from 320px up; body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, large text ≥3:1, placeholders held to the same body-text bar. Fully keyboard-accessible with visible focus rings and a logical tab order; all interactive controls labeled. High contrast maintained in both the light (paper) and dark (ink) sections. Content must never depend on JavaScript to become visible. Every motion path has a `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` alternative (crossfade or instant, no transform choreography). The embedded Google Calendar has a titled `<iframe>` and a text fallback so the agenda is never a dead end.
